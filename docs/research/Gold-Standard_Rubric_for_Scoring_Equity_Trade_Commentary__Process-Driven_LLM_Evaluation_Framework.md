# A gold-standard rubric for scoring equity trade commentary

The optimal rubric for scoring LLM-generated trade commentary combines **six independently scored dimensions** drawn from institutional finance practice, financial LLM evaluation research, and computational linguistics — deployed through a binary checklist methodology that achieves the highest inter-rater reliability between human experts and LLM judges. The core design principle: **process quality must dominate outcome quality**, with winning and losing trades evaluated on partially different criteria to prevent the model from learning outcome bias. This report synthesizes evidence from CFA Institute standards, Valentine's industry-adopted frameworks (now embedded in CFA Level II curriculum), the FinReasoning benchmark's 4×12 expert rubric (83.7% human-LLM agreement), CheckEval's binary decomposition method (+0.45 inter-evaluator agreement improvement), and König-Kersting's experimental proof that outcome bias contaminates process evaluation even when full information is available.

## What institutional desks actually reward in analyst writing

The clearest articulation of institutional quality comes from James Valentine's frameworks, developed during his tenure as Morgan Stanley's Director of Training and Product Quality overseeing 500+ analysts. His **FaVeS™ framework** defines the minimum bar for a publishable stock call: every recommendation must contain an out-of-consensus view in at least one of three domains — **Forecast** (financial estimates superior to consensus), **Valuation** (methodology or multiple the market is misapplying), or **Sentiment** (an anticipated shift in investor positioning). "If you don't have an out-of-consensus view in one of these three areas, you don't have a stock call worth making." This principle translates directly to trade commentary: the analysis must articulate *what the market is missing* about this specific pullback setup.

Valentine's communication quality test, **CASCADE™**, provides the structural scoring template. Elite commentary is **Conclusion-oriented** (leads with the trade thesis, not background), **Stock-oriented** (every fact connects to price action implications via explicit "so what"), **Concise**, **Aware** of consensus positioning, **Data-driven** with specific quantitative evidence, and **Easy to understand**. The CFA Institute formally adopted these frameworks into its curriculum in 2024, confirming their status as the institutional gold standard.

The distinction between junior and senior analyst writing is measurable. **Junior analysts** extrapolate past trends without cycle adjustment, use single valuation methods, bury conclusions after lengthy background, and confuse "cheap on P/E" with a genuine mispricing thesis. **Senior PMs** stress-test via upside/base/downside scenarios before taking positions, understand "what's in the price" through buy-side/sell-side dialogue, and maintain dispassionate discipline even when fighting the tape. A Stanford/Addepar survey of 54 institutional investors found that **97% use formal memo templates** and **78% require memo approval** before any investment, with the most critical section being a numerical scoring of the opportunity (minimum 6-7/10 to advance). Buy-side memos differ from sell-side in three measurable ways: they express dramatically stronger views (not consensus-hugging), treat risk as central rather than an afterthought, and quantify catalyst impact per share rather than listing generic growth drivers.

## The six scoring dimensions with anchored scales

Drawing from Trading-R1's three-stage reward decomposition (structure → evidence → decision), FinReasoning's 4-dimension expert rubric, and institutional quality frameworks, the rubric scores six dimensions. Each uses a **0–5 scale with explicit behavioral anchors** — the approach validated by Prometheus at 0.897 Pearson correlation with human experts.

**Dimension 1: Thesis clarity and differentiation (weight: 25%).** This is the highest-weighted dimension because institutional quality begins with a clear, falsifiable thesis. Score 1: restates obvious facts ("AAPL pulled back 5%") with no analytical value-add. Score 2: identifies the setup mechanically without explaining *why* this pullback is a buying opportunity. Score 3: articulates a clear thesis with entry logic, but the thesis could apply to any pullback in any stock — no stock-specific insight. Score 4: names what the market is mispricing about this specific stock, identifies a concrete catalyst within a defined timeframe, and explains why the pullback is noise rather than a trend change. Score 5: all of Score 4, plus quantifies the expected move, places the setup in the context of the stock's historical pattern behavior, and articulates what consensus is missing (Valentine's FaVeS™ test). The critical test at every level: **does the commentary pass the "so what" test** — is every fact explicitly connected to a trade implication?

**Dimension 2: Evidence grounding and quantitative density (weight: 20%).** Trading-R1's Stage II reward specifically targets evidence quality through an "opinion-quote-source" format — every claim must reference specific data. Score 1: purely qualitative assertions with no numbers. Score 2: includes some numbers but they're generic (market cap, last close) rather than analytically relevant. Score 3: cites relevant quantitative evidence (RSI levels, volume patterns, earnings multiples) but doesn't synthesize across data points. Score 4: integrates **multiple quantitative signals** into a coherent narrative — e.g., "RSI at 38 with volume declining 40% from the selloff peak, while the 50-day MA at $185 has held as support in 4 of the last 5 pullbacks." Score 5: all of Score 4, plus includes relative comparisons (vs. sector, vs. historical pattern, vs. implied volatility expectations) and explicitly notes data quality or limitations. Research from NYU Stern confirms that sentences containing numeric references have **24.6% lower Fog Index** scores — numbers improve clarity, not just rigor.

**Dimension 3: Risk identification and scenario framing (weight: 20%).** The buy-side/sell-side quality gap is widest here. Sell-side treats risk as an afterthought; buy-side makes it central. Score 1: no risk discussion whatsoever. Score 2: boilerplate risk language ("markets are volatile, past performance doesn't guarantee future results"). Score 3: names one or two genuine risks but doesn't quantify impact or probability. Score 4: identifies **the specific thesis-killer** — the single factor that would invalidate the trade — plus names 2-3 additional risks with estimated impact ranges. Includes a defined stop-loss or exit criterion. Score 5: presents explicit bull/base/bear scenarios with rough probability weights, identifies what information would cause a conviction change, and acknowledges base rates ("historically, ~X% of similar setups reach the target within the timeframe"). The CFA Institute Research Challenge allocates **15/100 points** specifically to investment risks, requiring "subjective risk quantification" — not just naming risks but sizing them.

**Dimension 4: Calibration and uncertainty expression (weight: 15%).** This dimension addresses the central tension the rubric must encode. The ZEW Financial Market Survey analyzing **40,000+ professional forecasts over 20 years** found persistent overprecision with no evidence of aggregate learning — professionals consistently set confidence intervals too narrow. The Loughran-McDonald financial sentiment dictionary provides the measurement tool: the **strong modal to weak modal ratio** (words like "will/shall/must/always" versus "could/may/might/possibly") serves as a quantitative overconfidence proxy. Score 1: all categorical certainty with zero hedging devices — "the stock will rally to $200." Score 2: includes weak hedging but it's boilerplate that adds no information ("results may vary"). Score 3: uses appropriate epistemic markers ("likely," "we expect," "our base case") but doesn't ground confidence in evidence quality. Score 4: explicitly links conviction level to evidence strength — "based on the strong technical support at $185 (tested 4 times this year) combined with accelerating institutional accumulation, we assign moderate-to-high conviction to this setup." Score 5: all of Score 4, plus uses conditional reasoning ("if the stock fails to hold $182 within 3 sessions, the thesis is invalidated"), acknowledges what's genuinely unknowable, and maintains the Friedman calibration framework — assessing reliability of evidence, breadth of reasonable alternatives, and sensitivity to new information.

**Dimension 5: Structural quality and communication clarity (weight: 10%).** Trading-R1's Stage I reward focuses entirely on structural compliance, and FinReasoning's Track 3 explicitly scores "structural rigor." Score 1: stream-of-consciousness with no logical organization. Score 2: has some structure but buries the conclusion. Score 3: leads with the thesis (CASCADE™ compliance), follows a logical sequence, but transitions are mechanical. Score 4: clean thesis-first structure with natural flow from setup identification → evidence → risk → trade parameters → expected outcome. Sentence length averages **15-25 words** (the optimal range identified in readability research). Score 5: all of Score 4, plus uses causal connectives ("because," "therefore," "driven by") and counter-argument markers ("although," "despite") to build a genuine analytical narrative rather than a list of observations. Active voice exceeds 60%. Research on fraudulent vs. non-fraudulent financial disclosures found that low-quality text uses more words to communicate less actual content — **information density per sentence** is a reliable quality signal.

**Dimension 6: Temporal reasoning and trade mechanics (weight: 10%).** This dimension is specific to short-swing pullback commentary and doesn't appear in general financial evaluation frameworks. Score 1: no timeframe specified for the trade. Score 2: vague temporal reference ("short-term opportunity"). Score 3: specifies a holding period but doesn't connect it to the technical setup's typical resolution window. Score 4: defines entry criteria, target, stop, and timeframe, all grounded in the specific setup's historical behavior pattern — "targeting $195 within 5-8 sessions, based on the stock's median recovery time from similar pullbacks to the 50-day MA." Score 5: all of Score 4, plus includes conditional time-based logic ("if the stock hasn't reclaimed $188 by day 3, reduce position by half") and relates the timeframe to upcoming catalysts or event risk.

## Deploying the rubric through LLM-as-judge at scale

The research strongly favors **absolute scoring with binary checklist decomposition** over Likert scales or pairwise comparisons for this use case. CheckEval's methodology — decomposing quality dimensions into yes/no questions — improves inter-evaluator agreement by **0.45** and reduces score variance dramatically compared to direct Likert scoring. For evaluating thousands of training examples independently, absolute scoring is also more robust to manipulation: pairwise preferences flip in ~35% of cases when distractor features are introduced, versus only ~9% for absolute scores.

The implementation should follow the **Prometheus prompt architecture**, which achieves 0.897 Pearson correlation with human judges: Task Description → Commentary to Evaluate → Reference Answer (Score 5 example) → Score Rubric (with all 5 levels described) → Require Feedback before Score. The critical additions for financial domain reliability are threefold. First, **require chain-of-thought reasoning before each score** — this improves accuracy by 5-15% across all studies. Second, **separate numerical accuracy checking** from qualitative assessment; use programmatic verification for figures, ratios, and calculations, reserving the LLM judge for narrative and analytical quality. Third, **include 3-5 domain-specific calibration examples** spanning the full score range within the prompt.

For each of the six dimensions, the checklist should contain **3-5 binary questions** that decompose the dimension into verifiable attributes. For example, Dimension 1 (Thesis Clarity) decomposes into: "Does the commentary state a specific, actionable trade thesis in the first two sentences? [Yes/No]" — "Does it explain what the market is mispricing or missing about this setup? [Yes/No]" — "Does it identify a specific catalyst or timeframe for the thesis to play out? [Yes/No]" — "Is the thesis falsifiable — could you clearly determine if it was wrong? [Yes/No]." The final dimension score equals the proportion of "Yes" answers, mapped to the 0-5 scale.

**Temperature must be set to 0** for deterministic outputs. Use a different model family as judge than as generator to avoid self-enhancement bias (Claude judging Qwen3 outputs satisfies this). Calibrate against **50 human-expert-labeled examples** before deployment, targeting Cohen's κ ≥ 0.6 per question — the threshold where LLM-as-judge reliability matches or exceeds typical human inter-annotator agreement on subjective financial text quality. Re-calibrate monthly with a fresh 20-sample gold set and track score distributions for drift.

## Handling the win/loss asymmetry without teaching the model to "result"

This is the rubric's hardest design problem. König-Kersting et al.'s 2021 experiments demonstrated that evaluators rate **the identical decision process more positively** when the random outcome is favorable — and this bias persists even when evaluators have complete process information. The implication is severe: any rubric that doesn't explicitly counteract outcome bias will contaminate the training data with exactly the pattern the model should avoid learning.

Annie Duke's 2×2 matrix provides the structural solution. Every training example maps to one of four quadrants: **good process / good outcome** (standard winner — reinforce), **good process / bad outcome** (well-analyzed loss — highest learning value and often the most underrepresented quadrant), **bad process / good outcome** (lucky win — must score LOW despite profit), and **bad process / bad outcome** (standard loser). The training dataset must contain adequate representation across all four quadrants, with the "good process / bad outcome" quadrant deliberately upweighted because it teaches the model the hardest lesson: that excellent analysis can accompany a losing trade.

Professional traders use a **Type A/B/C loss classification** that the rubric should encode. A Type A loss (followed all rules, market didn't cooperate) represents normal variance and should score HIGH on process dimensions. A Type B loss (valid setup, poor execution — entered late, moved stop, exited emotionally) reflects moderate process failure. A Type C loss (FOMO, revenge trade, no edge) represents complete process failure. The critical scoring rule: **a winning trade with poor process commentary must score lower than a losing trade with excellent process commentary.** This single rule, consistently enforced, prevents the model from learning outcome bias.

The rubric achieves this through **temporal separation in scoring**. The first five dimensions (thesis, evidence, risk, calibration, structure) are scored **process-blind** — evaluating analytical quality without reference to whether the trade profited. Only after these scores are locked does a sixth, outcome-conditional overlay apply. For winning trades, this overlay checks for **anti-resulting honesty** (does the commentary acknowledge lucky factors?), **unrealized risk acknowledgment** (does it discuss risks that didn't materialize but could have?), and **repeatability assessment** (is this a process worth repeating?). For losing trades, the overlay checks for **process-outcome separation** (does the commentary explicitly distinguish process quality from result?), **loss classification** (is the loss categorized as strategy/execution/emotional?), **thesis validity assessment** (was the thesis wrong or was timing off?), and **actionable learning extraction** (are specific, testable process changes identified?).

Mauboussin's framework grounds this design in decision theory: investing sits heavily on the luck side of the skill-luck continuum, meaning short-term results **cannot distinguish between good and bad processes**. TDAM's semi-annual post-mortem framework operationalizes this at the institutional level — they conduct December exercises focused specifically on critiquing one's own decision-making process, with the explicit cultural rule that "the goal should be to dissect the decision, not to murder the decision maker." The rubric should reward commentary that demonstrates this same analytical maturity.

## What the best financial LLM evaluation research reveals about gaps

The most directly applicable evaluation framework in the financial LLM literature is **FinReasoning's Track 3 "Deep Insight" rubric**, which scores across four dimensions — justification and causal depth, evidential grounding, comparative insight, and structural rigor — using 12 indicators evaluated by an LLM judge with **83.7% agreement** against expert human reviewers. Trading-R1's three-stage reward decomposition (format compliance → evidence quality → decision alignment) provides the curriculum design insight: train structure first, then evidence grounding, then decision quality, in sequence.

However, a significant gap exists: **no published financial LLM paper provides explicit score-level descriptions** (what does a 1 vs. 5 look like?) for trade commentary quality. Most evaluation is accuracy-based (correct/incorrect numerical answers) rather than quality-based (how well was the reasoning articulated). The FinBen benchmark's 42-dataset framework organizes evaluation by cognitive spectrum — from basic extraction through generation to strategic decision-making — but doesn't provide rubric text for narrative quality. This rubric fills that gap by combining institutional quality standards (which define *what* excellence looks like) with ML evaluation methodology (which defines *how* to measure it reliably at scale).

The Loughran-McDonald financial sentiment dictionary provides the **strongest validated toolkit** for computational quality measurement: six sentiment categories calibrated across 50,000+ financial filings. For this rubric, the most actionable metrics are the uncertainty word density (from their ~285-word uncertainty lexicon), the strong-to-weak modal ratio, and the presence of forward-looking statement markers. These can serve as automated pre-filters before the more expensive LLM-as-judge evaluation, flagging commentary that has zero uncertainty words in 200+ words (likely overconfident) or Fog Index above 20 (likely obfuscating rather than communicating).

## Conclusion

Three design principles emerged as non-negotiable from this research. First, **process scoring must be temporally separated from outcome awareness** — score the five process dimensions before the judge sees whether the trade won or lost, then apply outcome-conditional overlays. This is the only reliable way to prevent outcome bias from contaminating training data, given König-Kersting's experimental proof that even informed evaluators cannot resist it. Second, **use binary checklist decomposition rather than direct Likert scoring** — CheckEval's +0.45 agreement improvement is too large to ignore when scoring thousands of examples where marginal reliability compounds into substantial data quality differences. Third, **the rubric must explicitly reward the rarest and most valuable training examples** — well-analyzed losses and honestly-assessed lucky wins — by scoring them higher than their more common counterparts (triumphalist wins and self-flagellating losses). The 2×2 process-outcome matrix should be used to audit training data composition, with the "good process / bad outcome" quadrant deliberately overrepresented relative to its natural frequency. These principles, combined with the six-dimension scoring framework and the LLM-as-judge deployment architecture described above, produce a rubric that encodes genuine institutional quality rather than surface-level pattern matching.
